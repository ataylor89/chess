# chess vocabulary

I thought I would make a file on chess vocabulary

The file on mathematics vocabulary is really fun, and really helpful, so I thought I would do the same for chess

Below, I'll define some words that are commonly used in the game of chess

| Word | Definition |
| ---- | ---------- |
| Best decision | The best possible decision |
| Best move | The best possible move |
| Check | A move that attacks the opponent's king |
| Checkmate | A check that cannot be escaped |
| Chess notation | A language for writing down chess moves and recording a game |
| Combination | (1) A combination of tactics (2) A sequence of moves that combines many tactics |
| Endgame | The third phase of a chess game, when queens have been traded and queens are no longer on the board, or when the pieces are so reduced that it gets classified as an endgame |
| En passant | En passant is a capture that a chess player can perform when the opponent's pawn advances two squares and their pawn is adjacent to the square that the opponent's pawn lands on |
| Intermezzo | An in-between move; an intermediate move; a synonym for zwischenzug |
| Lucena position | A theoretical endgame where white has a king on e8, a pawn on e7, and a rook on f1, and black has a king on g7 and a rook on a2. If it is white to move, then it is winning for white. If it is black to move, then it is a draw. In the Lucena position, the white pawn is on e7. In the Philidor, the white pawn is on e5. This is one way of distinguishing the Lucena from the Philidor. The Lucena position features an important technique called "creating a bridge". I think it is called "creating a bridge" because it creates a bridge to safety for the white king... it involves placing the white rook on a square that allows it to block checks. |
| Middlegame | The second phase of a chess game, after the players have finished their development |
| Opening | The first phase of a chess game, when both players develop their pieces |
| Philidor position | A theoretical endgame where white has a king on f5, a pawn on e5, and a rook on a7, and black has a king on e8 and a rook on b1. It was long thought that the position was winning for white if it is white to move, but it's actually drawn. Black can draw the position if it is black to move, and black can also draw the position if it is white to move. When it is black to move, the draw is more straightforward. It's worth mentioning that horizontal checks are a theme in the Lucena, and vertical checks are a theme in the Philidor. I like to say that black has a horizontal check strategy in the Lucena and a vertical check strategy in the Philidor. The vertical check strategy might not be relevant right away, but it becomes relevant after the white pawn advances to e6, because the white king has no refuge from checks. If the white king strays too far from the pawn, then black can win the pawn by putting their rook behind the pawn. |
| Positional chess | (1) Evaluating positions (2) An approach to studying chess where a student learns chess by studying key positions (the positions could be taken from the opening, the middlegame, or the endgame) (3) Strategy |
| Strategy | (1) A plan (2) The part of chess concerned with planning (3) Positional chess |
| Tactic | (1) A move that wins the game (2) A winning move (3) A move that achieves a desired outcome (e.g. a checkmate, a material advantage, or a desirable draw) (4) A move; an attack; a type of attack |
| Zugzwang | A player is in zugzwang when every move weakens their position; a player is in zugzwang when every move loses |
| Zwischenzug | An in-between move; an intermediate move; a synonym for intermezzo |
